The 48 Laws of Power

By Robert Greene - Timeless lessons about power, strategy, and human nature.

Law 1: Never Outshine the Master

Always make those above you feel comfortably superior. In your desire to please or impress them, do not go too far in displaying your talents or you might accomplish the opposite—inspire fear and insecurity.

Law 2: Never Put Too Much Trust in Friends

Friends will more quickly betray you because they are easily aroused to envy. But hire a former enemy and he will be more loyal than a friend, because he has more to prove.

Law 3: Conceal Your Intentions

Keep people off-balance and in the dark by never revealing the purpose behind your actions. If they have no clue what you are up to, they cannot prepare a defense.

Law 4: Always Say Less Than Necessary

When you are trying to impress people with words, the more you say, the more common you appear, and the less in control. Even if you are saying something banal, it will seem original if you make it vague, open-ended, and sphinxlike.

Ray Dalio's Principles

Life and work principles from the founder of Bridgewater Associates.

Embrace Reality and Deal with It

There is nothing more important than understanding how reality works and how to deal with it. The state of mind you bring to this process makes all the difference.

Be Radically Transparent

Radical transparency and radical open-mindedness are invaluable for rapid learning and effective change.

Look to Nature for How Reality Works

All the laws of reality were given to us by nature. Man didn't create these laws, but by understanding them we can use them to foster our own evolution.

Believability-Weight Your Decision Making

Consider the track record and believability of people giving you advice. Weight their opinions accordingly.

Buddha's Four Noble Truths

The foundation of Buddhist philosophy and the path to enlightenment.

The Truth of Suffering (Dukkha)

Life inherently contains suffering, dissatisfaction, and impermanence. Birth, aging, illness, death, and separation from what we love are all forms of suffering.

The Truth of the Cause of Suffering

Suffering arises from attachment, craving, and desire. Our constant wanting and clinging to things, people, and experiences creates suffering.

The Truth of the End of Suffering

It is possible to end suffering by eliminating attachment and craving. This state is called Nirvana.

The Truth of the Path

The Eightfold Path leads to the end of suffering: Right Understanding, Right Intention, Right Speech, Right Action, Right Livelihood, Right Effort, Right Mindfulness, Right Concentration.

Bushido Code - Way of the Warrior

The moral code of the samurai, emphasizing honor, courage, and loyalty.

Gi (義) - Rectitude

Be acutely honest throughout your dealings with all people. Believe in justice, not from other people, but from yourself.

Yu (勇) - Courage

Hiding like a turtle in a shell is not living at all. A true warrior must have heroic courage and face life completely.

Jin (仁) - Benevolence

Through intense training and hard work the true warrior becomes quick and strong. Be kind to others and help those in need.

Rei (礼) - Respect

True warriors have no reason to be cruel. They do not need to prove their strength. Respect for others is the mark of true nobility.

Charlie Munger's Mental Models

Thinking tools and principles from Warren Buffett's partner at Berkshire Hathaway.

Inversion

Instead of thinking forward, think backward. Consider what you want to avoid and work backwards from there.

Circle of Competence

Know what you know and what you don't know. Stay within your circle of competence and be honest about its boundaries.

First Principles Thinking

Break down complicated problems into basic elements and then reassemble them from the ground up.

Multidisciplinary Thinking

Use models from different disciplines to better understand complex problems. Avoid the man-with-a-hammer syndrome.

The Art of War

Sun Tzu's timeless principles of strategy and warfare, applicable to business and life.

Know Yourself and Your Enemy

If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles.

Win Without Fighting

Supreme excellence consists of breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting.

Speed is the Essence of War

Rapidity is the essence of war: take advantage of the enemy's unreadiness, make your way by unexpected routes, and attack unguarded spots.

Adaptability is Key

All warfare is based on deception. Be flexible and adapt your tactics to the situation. Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground.

Stoic Philosophy

Ancient wisdom from Marcus Aurelius, Seneca, and Epictetus on living virtuously.

Focus on What You Can Control

Concentrate your energy on actions within your power and accept what you cannot change. This is the foundation of inner peace.

Virtue is the Only True Good

External circumstances cannot truly harm you. Character and virtue are the only things that matter.

Memento Mori - Remember Death

Death is inevitable and can come at any time. This awareness makes every moment precious and focuses us on what truly matters.

Amor Fati - Love Your Fate

Embrace everything that happens to you, both good and bad. Turn obstacles into fuel for growth and wisdom.

View from Above

Imagine yourself from a cosmic perspective. Your troubles are small in the grand scheme of things. This brings tranquility and perspective.

Premeditatio Malorum

Visualize potential loss or setbacks to build resilience and appreciate what you have. This mental exercise reduces anxiety about the future.

The Discipline of Desire

Want what happens, not what you wish would happen. Align your desires with reality to achieve contentment.

Present Moment Awareness

The past is gone, the future uncertain. Focus your attention on the present moment where your power to act exists.

High Output Management

Andrew S. Grove's principles for effective management and leadership.

Output-Oriented Management

Focus on results, not activities. A manager's output is the output of their organization and neighboring organizations under their influence.

One-on-Ones Are Essential

Regular one-on-one meetings are the manager's most important tool for maintaining relationships and staying informed.

Task-Relevant Maturity

Adjust your management style based on the employee's experience with the specific task. High TRM needs delegation, low TRM needs close supervision.

Leverage Your Time

Maximize your output by focusing on high-leverage activities: information gathering, decision making, nudging others, and being a role model.

Meetings Are a Medium

Meetings are not inherently good or bad - they're a medium. Use process-oriented, mission-oriented, and one-on-one meetings strategically.

Performance Reviews Drive Performance

Regular, structured performance reviews are critical. They should assess past performance and focus on improving future output.

Lean Startup

Eric Ries's methodology for building successful startups through validated learning.

Build-Measure-Learn

Turn ideas into products, measure customer response, and learn whether to pivot or persevere. Minimize time through this feedback loop.

Validated Learning

Progress in startups is measured by validated learning about customers. Test assumptions with real market data.

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

Build the simplest version of your product that allows a full turn of the Build-Measure-Learn loop with minimum effort and development time.

Innovation Accounting

Measure progress in the face of extreme uncertainty. Use actionable metrics, not vanity metrics, to make decisions.

Zen Philosophy

Timeless wisdom from Zen Buddhism emphasizing mindfulness, simplicity, and direct experience.

Beginner's Mind (Shoshin)

Approach everything with openness, eagerness, and freedom from preconceptions. In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, in the expert's mind there are few.

Non-Attachment

Release attachment to outcomes, possessions, and even thoughts. Freedom comes from letting go, not from holding on.

Mindful Awareness

Be fully present in each moment. Pay attention to what is happening now, without judgment or the need to change anything.

Embrace Impermanence

Everything changes constantly. Accept the transient nature of all things. This understanding brings peace and reduces suffering.

Direct Experience Over Concepts

Truth is found through direct experience, not through theories or concepts. Practice and experience wisdom rather than just studying it.

Simplicity (Kanso)

Eliminate clutter and complexity from your life. Find beauty and peace in simplicity. Less is often more.

Japanese Life Principles

Profound concepts from Japanese culture that guide a meaningful and balanced life.

Ikigai (生き甲斐) - Reason for Being

Find your purpose at the intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. This is your reason for getting up in the morning.

Kaizen (改善) - Continuous Improvement

Make small, incremental improvements consistently over time. Even tiny changes compound into remarkable transformations. Progress, not perfection.

Wabi-Sabi (侘寂) - Beauty in Imperfection

Find beauty in imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness. Accept the natural cycle of growth and decay. Embrace flaws as part of life's authentic beauty.

Mono no Aware (物の哀れ) - Bittersweet Awareness

Appreciate the transient nature of life. Feel the poignant awareness that all things are temporary, which makes them more precious and beautiful.

Omotenashi (おもてなし) - Selfless Service

Provide wholeheartedly selfless service without expecting anything in return. Anticipate others' needs and serve with genuine care and respect.

Shikata ga Nai (仕方がない) - Acceptance

"It cannot be helped." Accept circumstances beyond your control with grace. Focus energy on what you can influence rather than lamenting what you cannot change.

Oubaitori (桜梅桃李) - Don't Compare

Each flower blooms in its own time. Don't compare your journey to others. The cherry, plum, peach, and damson all bloom beautifully in their unique way and season.

Forest Bathing (森林浴) - Shinrin-yoku

Immerse yourself mindfully in nature to reduce stress and improve well-being. Breathe deeply, observe quietly, and let the forest heal your mind and spirit.

Financial Principles

Essential numeric rules and economic concepts for wealth building and financial wisdom.

The 80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle)

80% of results come from 20% of causes. 80% of wealth comes from 20% of investments. Focus your energy on the vital few that drive most outcomes.

Rule of 72

Divide 72 by your annual return rate to find how long it takes your money to double. At 8% return, your money doubles in 9 years (72 ÷ 8 = 9).

The 50/30/20 Budget Rule

Allocate 50% of after-tax income to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt repayment. This creates a balanced financial foundation.

The 4% Withdrawal Rule

Withdraw 4% of your retirement portfolio annually to maintain your wealth indefinitely. This rule helps determine how much you need to retire comfortably.

Law of Diminishing Returns

Adding more of one input while holding others constant eventually yields progressively smaller increases in output. More effort doesn't always mean proportionally better results.

Opportunity Cost

The value of the best alternative you give up when making a choice. Every decision has a hidden cost - what you could have done instead.

Price vs. Value

"Price is what you pay, value is what you get." - Warren Buffett. Focus on intrinsic value, not market price. Sometimes the best investments look expensive but offer great value.

The Rule of 110

Subtract your age from 110 to determine what percentage should be in stocks. A 30-year-old should have 80% in stocks (110 - 30 = 80). Adjust risk as you age.

The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People

Stephen Covey's timeless principles for personal and professional effectiveness.

1. Be Proactive

Take responsibility for your life. Focus your efforts on things you can influence rather than reactive worrying about conditions over which you have little or no control.

2. Begin with the End in Mind

Define clear measures of success and a plan to achieve them. Start each day with a clear understanding of your desired direction and destination.

3. Put First Things First

Prioritize and do the most important things first. Organize and execute around priorities. Focus on important activities, not just urgent ones.

4. Think Win-Win

Seek mutual benefit in all human interactions. Life is a cooperative arena, not a competitive one. Win-Win is a frame of mind and heart.

5. Seek First to Understand, Then to be Understood

Listen with the intent to understand, not the intent to reply. Most people listen with the intent to reply, not to understand.

6. Synergize

Combine the strengths of people through positive teamwork. The whole is greater than the sum of its parts. Value differences and build on strengths.

7. Sharpen the Saw

Renew yourself regularly in four areas: physical, mental, emotional/social, and spiritual. Continuous improvement and renewal preserve and enhance your greatest asset—you.
